VI. Optimization of a trip through Mexico City

April 14, 2019

Mexico is a country that, over time, has been gaining importance in the tourism sector. In fact, currently, and for several years now, tourism is considered one of the main engines of growth and job creation for the country, as well as one of the most important economic sectors. To exemplify this, it is appropriate to analyze certain data. According to the United Nations World Tourism Organization (UNWTO), in 2019, Mexico ranked seventh among countries with the highest number of tourist arrivals, with a total of 45 million. In 2020, it rose to third place on the list, with 24.3 million (a figure affected by Covid-19). Finally, in 2021, the country ranked second, with 31.9 million tourists, only behind France.

As is known, tourism is an important source of economic contribution for any country. For Mexico, in 2019, foreign tourism brought in 24.6 billion dollars. For the following year, the foreign exchange income was 10.996 billion dollars, and finally, for 2021, it reached 19.8 billion dollars.

Regarding domestic tourism, in the country, efforts have been made to promote it by implementing initiatives such as the “Pueblos Mágicos” (Magical Towns), places where a significant portion of domestic tourism is concentrated. According to the Ministry of Tourism’s annual publication of the Statistical Compendium of Tourism in Mexico (2018), there were 100.4 million arrivals of domestic tourists to hotels, and when adding the arrival of other travelers to different types of accommodations, an estimated 551.4 million residents of Mexico traveled. All this generated a consumption of 2.7 trillion pesos, with accommodation and transportation being the most significant sectors.

It is also important to mention that in Mexico, since 2012, employment in the tourism sector has been growing by approximately 3% each year, and by 2018, it was stated that 10 million workers depended on tourism, either directly or indirectly [SECTUR, 2018].

All the data mentioned above are the result of Mexico having an endless number of tourist destinations that are attractive to both foreigners and locals. Some of them, as mentioned earlier, are the “Pueblos Mágicos” program, which was created in 2001 with the aim of giving significant value to towns with rich heritage. As of today, there are 132 of them. However, in addition to these, there are other destinations that are known for their tourist and cultural popularity, including Mexico City, Oaxaca, Chiapas, Cancun, Guanajuato, Chichen Itza, Teotihuacan, Yucatan, and Palenque [SECTUR, 2015].

Problematic

Tourists, when planning a trip, often rely on certain information to decide their destination. Over time, the internet has become an essential tool as it provides access to information about hotels, airlines, agencies, tourist guides, maps, and even forums or platforms that encourage interaction among travelers for the exchange of experiences and opinions.

Carrying out good advance planning is crucial as it will determine how the trip unfolds. Typically, tourists base their decisions on their budget, which influences the choice of destination and duration of the trip. Additionally, comparing transportation options and hotels is important, considering the locations they wish to visit. These choices may depend on the type of trip they want to experience, but typically include popular or central places in the city. Therefore, having a reliable resource that provides access to this information is necessary when planning and during the trip itself.

Taking this into account, the idea is proposed to find a means through which tourists can plan their trips by defining the places they want to visit, considering any existing restrictions and their preferences. Often, tourists are not aware of the possible routes they can take, so it would be beneficial to provide them with a variety of options based on their financial resources and time constraints.

Preferences and Restrictions

Preferences and Restrictions

To address the optimization of trips and provide more suitable responses to tourists, it is important to understand their preferences and restrictions. The following are the most relevant preferences to consider when solving optimization problems in this context:

  • Budget: The cost of the trip is a crucial factor to consider when providing an itinerary that fits the traveler’s budget.
  • Activities: It is important to take into account the traveler’s preferences regarding the activities they want to engage in during their trip. Suggestions can be made for activities that align with their interests.
  • Accommodation: The type and quality of accommodation can have a significant impact on the traveler’s experience.
  • Gastronomy: The model can consider the traveler’s culinary preferences and suggest restaurants or gastronomic routes that suit their tastes.
  • Language: The traveler’s language preferences can be taken into account to suggest destinations where they can comfortably communicate and make the most of their trip.
  • Climate: The traveler’s climate preferences can be considered to suggest destinations where they can enjoy their preferred weather conditions.

In addition, when addressing trip optimization problems, it is important to consider the following restrictions:

  • Time restrictions: The available time for the trip is a crucial factor, as the itinerary needs to fit within this time frame.
  • Distance and transportation: The distances between attractions and the availability of transportation to move between them are important restrictions to consider.
  • Travel restrictions: Visa requirements, restrictions related to the COVID-19 situation, or other legal restrictions may affect the traveler’s ability to visit certain destinations. Current travel regulations should be taken into account.
  • Accessibility: The model should consider the accessibility of destinations and attractions, particularly for travelers with disabilities or mobility issues.
  • Health and safety: The model should consider the traveler’s health and safety when suggesting destinations and activities, especially in areas with high crime rates or a high risk of natural disasters.
  • Cultural or religious restrictions: The model should consider any cultural or religious restrictions that may affect the traveler’s ability to visit certain destinations or participate in certain activities.

By considering these preferences and restrictions, the model can provide a more personalized itinerary that takes into account all the factors important to the traveler.

My Problem

Knowing that Mexico City is one of the most visited cities in the country, the objective of the problem is to find a way to optimize the routes and daily stops in order to enhance the visitor’s experience and promote tourism in the country. This will be achieved by using the SIMPLEX method and utilizing integer programming as a tool to optimize the presented scenario.

It is important to clarify that the problem is presented as the “perfect trip,” considering the 12 most attractive and culturally rich points that the city has to offer. The optimization focuses solely on the travel time between each point, assuming all transfers will be made by car. Additionally, the trip duration is set to 5 days, as it is considered the perfect amount of time to explore Mexico City according to various sources.

One limitation of the established method is that the CVRP model may not be able to solve problems with more than 30 nodes. However, it is applicable in this specific project since a normal tourist trip would not typically involve visiting more than that number of attractions.
